Remote Sensing Quiz 3

Based on Remote Sensing B Invitational Topics • 20 Questions • Division B Prep

Section 1 — EM Spectrum & Wave Properties

1. Which two types of electromagnetic radiation are immediately adjacent to visible light on the EM spectrum?

  • Microwaves and ultraviolet
  • Infrared and ultraviolet
  • Radio waves and infrared
  • Ultraviolet and X-rays

2. Which electromagnetic wave has the highest frequency?

  • X-rays
  • Ultraviolet
  • Gamma rays
  • Microwaves

3. Rank the following wavelengths from smallest to largest: X-rays, red light, microwaves, radio waves.

  • X-rays, red, microwaves, radio waves
  • Red, X-rays, microwaves, radio waves
  • Microwaves, red, X-rays, radio waves
  • Radio waves, microwaves, red, X-rays

4. How many possible pixel values does an 8-bit resolution image have?

  • 128
  • 256
  • 512
  • 16

5. Spectral emissions are primarily used for:

  • Measuring deforestation rates
  • Mapping ocean floor bathymetry
  • Measuring background radiation levels
  • Determining elemental composition of materials

Answers — Section 1

  1. B — Infrared (lower energy) and ultraviolet (higher energy) are on either side of visible light.
  2. C — Gamma rays have the shortest wavelength and highest frequency of all EM radiation.
  3. A — X-rays (~0.01–10 nm) < red light (~620–750 nm) < microwaves (~1 mm–1 m) < radio waves (>1 m).
  4. B — 2⁸ = 256 possible values (0 to 255).
  5. D — Emission spectra reveal the unique spectral fingerprint of elements, enabling compositional analysis.

Section 2 — Sensors & Imaging

6. A new satellite maps crops at night by sending short pulses of microwave radiation and measuring the return signal. This sensor is best described as:

  • Passive sensor using visible light
  • Passive sensor using infrared radiation
  • Active sensor using microwave radiation
  • Active sensor using ultraviolet radiation

7. Why is vegetation often shown as red in false-color composite images?

  • Vegetation naturally emits red light
  • Near-infrared light (strongly reflected by vegetation) is displayed in the red channel
  • Red light penetrates canopy better than other wavelengths
  • Chlorophyll absorbs red light, making it appear brighter

8. Radiometric correction of remote sensing data is primarily used to:

  • Remove sensor and atmospheric effects so pixel values represent true surface reflectance
  • Correct distortions in the geometric shape and position of features
  • Mosaic multiple images together into a seamless composite
  • Change the pixel size of an image

9. What does NDVI stand for?

  • Normalised Distance Vegetation Indicator
  • Normalised Difference Vegetation Index
  • Near-infrared Differential Vegetation Instrument
  • Natural Density Vegetation Index

10. What type of sensor does the Landsat satellite series primarily use?

  • Active radar (SAR)
  • Multispectral sensors
  • Single-band panchromatic only
  • Microwave radiometers

Answers — Section 2

  1. C — The satellite emits its own microwave energy and measures the return — an active microwave sensor.
  2. B — In color infrared (CIR) composites, the NIR band is assigned to the red display channel, making healthy vegetation appear red.
  3. A — Radiometric correction removes sensor noise and atmospheric scattering/absorption to recover true surface reflectance.
  4. B — Normalised Difference Vegetation Index: NDVI = (NIR − Red) / (NIR + Red).
  5. B — Landsat carries multispectral sensors (OLI/TIRS on Landsat 8/9) covering visible through thermal IR bands.

Section 3 — Orbits & Satellite Operations

11. Which of the following altitudes is considered low Earth orbit (LEO)?

  • 2,010 km above the surface
  • 10,000 km above the surface
  • 4,500 km above the surface
  • 1,000 km above the surface

12. What is the range for medium Earth orbit (MEO)?

  • 2,000 km – 4,500 km
  • 1,000 km – 2,500 km
  • 2,000 km – 35,500 km
  • 100 km – 215 km

13. A geosynchronous orbit (GSO) occurs at what altitude above the equator?

  • 35,786 km
  • 34,786 km
  • 35,000 km
  • 20,000 km

14. A satellite stays above the same point on the equator, sending images every 15 minutes to track storm clouds. Which orbit and application best fits this?

  • Low Earth orbit; detailed local topographic mapping with LiDAR
  • Geostationary orbit; continuous weather monitoring of large regions
  • Polar orbit; mapping small city parks once a year
  • Sun-synchronous orbit; measuring building heights with radar altimetry

15. What is a geosynchronous orbit?

  • An orbit where the satellite’s speed matches the rotation speed of the ground beneath it
  • An orbit around Earth whose orbital period equals Earth’s rotation period
  • An orbit where the satellite appears fixed above one point on the equator
  • An orbit in which the satellite experiences more than 1 g of gravity

Answers — Section 3

  1. D — LEO is generally defined as 160–2,000 km; 1,000 km falls within this range.
  2. C — MEO spans from ~2,000 km to ~35,500 km (below geostationary altitude).
  3. A — The geostationary altitude is 35,786 km above the equator.
  4. B — Geostationary satellites remain fixed above one point, ideal for continuous weather monitoring.
  5. B — A geosynchronous orbit has a period equal to Earth’s rotation (~24 hours). Note: geostationary is a special case with 0° inclination.

Section 4 — Environmental Science & Applications

16. Which type of UV radiation does the ozone layer primarily block?

  • UV-C only
  • UV-A and UV-B
  • UV-B and UV-C
  • UV-C and UV-A

17. The chemical equation H₂O + CO₂ → H₂CO₃ describes:

  • Photosynthesis in marine algae
  • The formation of acid rain from sulfur dioxide
  • Ocean acidification from dissolved CO₂
  • Carbonate rock weathering on land

18. A land-cover map from Landsat shows that over 10 years, many green “forest” pixels near a city change to gray “urban” pixels. Which conclusion is most supported?

  • The city’s air pollution has decreased
  • The spatial resolution is too coarse to detect any land-cover change
  • The elevation of the city has increased by 30 metres
  • Urban expansion has reduced forested habitat near the city

19. Over 20 years, multispectral images show a bright, snow-covered area changing into darker forest as climate warms. How does this affect Earth’s energy balance?

  • Albedo increases, so more sunlight is reflected and the surface cools
  • Albedo stays the same because trees and snow reflect equally
  • Albedo decreases, causing less solar energy to reach the surface
  • Albedo decreases, so the darker surface absorbs more solar energy and warms

20. Which type of greenhouse gas has the highest Global Warming Potential (GWP)?

  • Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂)
  • Methane (CH₄)
  • Nitrous oxide (N₂O)

Answers — Section 4

  1. C — The ozone layer blocks UV-B (partially) and virtually all UV-C radiation.
  2. C — CO₂ dissolves in seawater forming carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), the key reaction in ocean acidification.
  3. D — The conversion of forest pixels to urban pixels over time demonstrates urban sprawl reducing nearby forest.
  4. D — Snow (high albedo) replaced by darker forest (lower albedo) means more solar absorption and warming — the snow-albedo feedback.
  5. A — CFCs have extremely high GWP values (thousands to tens of thousands times that of CO₂).
