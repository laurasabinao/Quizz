Remote Sensing Quiz 1

Based on MIT Invitational Remote Sensing C Topics • 20 Questions • Division B Prep

Section 1 — Atmospheric Science & Spectroscopy

1. The infrared absorption of atmospheric gases at ~4.4 µm is dominated by which molecule?

  • Water vapor (H₂O)
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂)
  • Nitrous oxide (N₂O)
  • Methane (CH₄)

2. In the total atmospheric absorption spectrum, which substance is the dominant cause of absorption across broad infrared regions?

  • Ozone
  • Carbon dioxide
  • Water vapor
  • Nitrogen

3. Infrared absorption by water vapor is primarily caused by what molecular effect?

  • Electronic transitions
  • Nuclear spin flipping
  • Molecular vibration
  • Ionisation

4. OCO-2’s ability to measure atmospheric CO₂ is hindered by elevated aerosol concentrations. Why?

  • Aerosols absorb all CO₂ before it reaches the sensor
  • Aerosols scatter the signal, increasing measurement uncertainty
  • Aerosols chemically destroy CO₂ in the atmosphere
  • Aerosols block the satellite’s solar panels

5. MODIS uses its eighth band (405–420 nm) to identify areas of reduced reflectance from smoke plumes. This reduced reflectance is useful because it allows scientists to:

  • Directly measure CO₂ concentration through spectroscopy
  • Establish a relationship between smoke coverage and CO₂ emissions
  • Identify the chemical composition of burning vegetation
  • Map ocean chlorophyll beneath smoke plumes

Answers — Section 1

  1. C — Nitrous oxide (N₂O) has a strong absorption peak near 4.4 µm.
  2. C — Water vapor is the dominant absorber of infrared radiation in the atmosphere.
  3. C — Molecular vibration (bending and stretching modes) causes IR absorption in H₂O.
  4. B — Aerosols scatter the signal, introducing greater uncertainty in CO₂ retrievals.
  5. B — Reduced reflectance from smoke is correlated with CO₂ concentration, allowing indirect emission estimates.

Section 2 — Chlorophyll, Ocean Color & PACE

6. Chlorophyll-a absorbs light most strongly at approximately which two wavelengths?

  • 430 nm and 660 nm
  • 500 nm and 700 nm
  • 350 nm and 550 nm
  • 600 nm and 800 nm

7. Why does water with high chlorophyll concentration appear green rather than blue?

  • Chlorophyll emits green fluorescent light
  • Chlorophyll absorbs far more blue light than green light, shifting apparent color
  • High chlorophyll reflects all wavelengths equally
  • Green light penetrates deeper into chlorophyll-rich water

8. In ocean color remote sensing, the penetration depth (z90) is defined as the depth at which:

  • 90% of all phytoplankton reside
  • 90% of upwelling irradiance originates above
  • Water temperature drops by 90% of the surface value
  • Salinity reaches 90% of its maximum

9. Why is the PACE satellite placed in a Sun-synchronous orbit?

  • To remain stationary over the equator for continuous observation
  • To observe the same location at the same local time each day
  • To maximise its altitude for wider swath coverage
  • To follow the Moon’s gravitational path

10. In areas with a subsurface chlorophyll-a maxima layer (SCML), surface-only algorithms underestimate total Chl-a because:

  • Surface concentrations are always higher than subsurface
  • The SCML represents a decoupling of surface and subsurface concentrations
  • Satellites cannot detect any chlorophyll below 1 m depth
  • Phytoplankton migrate to the surface during satellite overpasses

Answers — Section 2

  1. A — Chlorophyll-a absorbs strongly at ~430 nm (blue) and ~660 nm (red).
  2. B — Chlorophyll absorbs ~1000× more blue light than green, leaving green as the dominant reflected color.
  3. B — z90 is the depth above which 90% of upwelling irradiance originates (or where downwelling irradiance is 10% of surface).
  4. B — Sun-synchronous orbits pass over locations at the same solar time daily, enabling consistent illumination for ocean color measurements.
  5. B — SCMLs decouple surface and subsurface Chl-a, so surface readings alone significantly underestimate the total water column.

Section 3 — Radiation, Climate & Energy Balance

11. An image showing how much solar energy is reflected by Earth’s surface and atmosphere is measuring:

  • Outgoing longwave radiation
  • Shortwave radiation (reflected sunlight)
  • Thermal infrared emission
  • Microwave backscatter

12. Bright areas near the equator in a reflected shortwave radiation map are caused by:

  • Desert sand reflecting sunlight
  • High levels of persistent cloud cover
  • Ocean surface glint
  • Volcanic ash

13. On an outgoing longwave radiation map, blue/purple regions at the equator most likely indicate:

  • Hot desert surfaces emitting maximum radiation
  • Cold high-altitude cloud tops from deep convection
  • Areas with no atmosphere
  • Regions of intense solar absorption

14. If Earth’s albedo is 0.3, what fraction of incoming solar radiation is absorbed by the Earth system?

  • 0.30
  • 0.50
  • 0.70
  • 1.00

15. Using the Stefan-Boltzmann law with an albedo of 0.3 and no greenhouse effect, Earth’s equilibrium temperature is approximately:

  • 288 K (15 °C)
  • 255 K (−18 °C)
  • 210 K (−63 °C)
  • 303 K (30 °C)

Answers — Section 3

  1. B — Reflected sunlight (shortwave radiation) is what determines albedo measurements.
  2. B — Equatorial bright areas on SW maps are from deep convective clouds (ITCZ) reflecting sunlight.
  3. B — Low OLR at the equator is from cold cloud tops of deep tropical convection (rainforest/ITCZ regions).
  4. C — If 30% is reflected (albedo = 0.3), then 70% is absorbed.
  5. B — The no-atmosphere equilibrium temperature with albedo 0.3 is approximately 255 K.

Section 4 — ENSO, Aerosols & Forest Fires

16. The positive feedback loop connecting sea surface temperatures, atmospheric convection, and trade winds during El Niño is called the:

  • Hadley feedback
  • Bjerknes feedback
  • Walker oscillation
  • Coriolis feedback

17. During a strong El Niño, what is one typical regional effect?

  • Increased rainfall in Indonesia and drought in Peru
  • Increased rainfall in Peru and drought in Indonesia/Australia
  • Global cooling due to increased cloud cover
  • Stronger Atlantic hurricane activity

18. If aerosol mitigation (reducing aerosol emissions) increases surface downwelling shortwave radiation, what is the expected effect on latent heat flux?

  • Latent heat flux decreases because less energy reaches the surface
  • Latent heat flux increases due to more energy driving evapotranspiration
  • Latent heat flux remains unchanged
  • Latent heat flux becomes negative

19. Increased latent heat from aerosol mitigation leads to more canopy transpiration. How does this affect forest fire susceptibility?

  • Decreases fire susceptibility because transpiration cools the forest
  • Increases fire susceptibility because drier soils result from greater transpiration
  • No effect on fire susceptibility
  • Decreases fire susceptibility because more rainfall is triggered

20. Recently burned areas can be identified from satellite imagery using increased surface temperature. Which MODIS bandwidth is most useful for detecting this thermal signal?

  • 0.4–0.5 µm (visible blue)
  • 0.6–0.7 µm (visible red)
  • 3.5–4.0 µm (shortwave IR)
  • 10–12 µm (thermal IR)

Answers — Section 4

  1. B — The Bjerknes feedback is the positive loop linking SSTs, convection, and trade winds in ENSO.
  2. B — El Niño brings increased rainfall/flooding to Peru and drought to Indonesia and Australia.
  3. B — More shortwave radiation reaching the surface drives greater evapotranspiration and latent heat flux.
  4. B — Greater transpiration depletes soil moisture, making forests more susceptible to fire.
  5. C — The 3.5–4.0 µm (SWIR) band is sensitive to elevated surface temperatures from burn scars.
