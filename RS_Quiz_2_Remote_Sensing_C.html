Remote Sensing Quiz 2

Based on Remote Sensing C Invitational Topics • 20 Questions • Division B Prep

Section 1 — Instruments & Satellites

1. Which of the following remote sensing instruments is passive?

  • Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR)
  • LiDAR altimeter
  • Infrared radiometer
  • Doppler weather radar

2. What is a key difference between LiDAR and radar?

  • LiDAR uses laser light; radar uses radio/microwave wavelengths
  • LiDAR is passive while radar is active
  • Radar is only space-based; LiDAR is only ground-based
  • Radar is unaffected by clouds; LiDAR penetrates all cloud types

3. CALIPSO is a satellite that uses laser pulses to profile atmospheric features. What does it primarily observe?

  • Sea surface temperature and salinity
  • Thin clouds and atmospheric aerosols in vertical cross-section
  • Precipitation rates inside thunderstorms
  • Land surface elevation changes

4. The GPM (Global Precipitation Measurement) mission carries which combination of instruments?

  • A spaceborne LiDAR and thermal camera
  • A pair of microwave radiometers in geostationary orbit
  • A dual-frequency precipitation radar and multi-channel microwave imager
  • An L-band radiometer and radar for soil moisture

5. ECOSTRESS, mounted on the International Space Station, primarily measures:

  • High-resolution land surface temperature and plant water use
  • Vertical profiles of stratospheric ozone
  • Ocean salinity patterns
  • Heat flow from Earth’s interior

Answers — Section 1

  1. C — An infrared radiometer detects naturally emitted radiation (passive). SAR, LiDAR, and Doppler radar all emit their own energy (active).
  2. A — LiDAR uses laser light (often near-IR or visible); radar uses radio/microwave wavelengths. Both are active systems.
  3. B — CALIPSO uses a space-based lidar (CALIOP) to create vertical profiles of thin clouds and aerosols.
  4. C — GPM carries a dual-frequency precipitation radar (DPR) and a GPM microwave imager (GMI) to measure 3D rain and snowfall.
  5. A — ECOSTRESS measures land surface temperature and evapotranspiration to assess plant water stress.

Section 2 — Orbits & Resolution

6. If a satellite’s orbit is raised from low Earth orbit to a much higher altitude, what happens to its orbital period and spatial resolution?

  • Period decreases; resolution improves
  • Period increases; resolution worsens
  • Both remain the same
  • Period decreases; resolution worsens

7. Which combination of factors primarily determines ground sample distance (GSD)?

  • Detector’s radiometric resolution and lens aperture
  • Satellite’s orbital inclination and period
  • Sensor’s instantaneous field of view (IFOV) and satellite altitude
  • Earth’s rotation rate and the satellite’s speed

8. Two satellites with identical cameras are at different altitudes: Satellite A at 700 km and Satellite B at 1,400 km. How do their GSDs compare?

  • Satellite B’s GSD is half of A’s
  • They have the same GSD
  • Satellite B’s GSD is twice that of A’s
  • GSD depends only on pixel size, not altitude

9. Which statement about geostationary orbits is correct?

  • Any orbit with a 24-hour period, regardless of inclination
  • A geosynchronous orbit in the equatorial plane with ~0° inclination
  • A sun-synchronous orbit that passes over the equator at noon daily
  • A low-altitude orbit that circles both poles every 90 minutes

10. Why might microwave sensors for precipitation measurement be placed in polar orbits rather than geostationary orbits?

  • Polar orbits are cheaper to launch into
  • Microwave sensors need to be close to the surface for adequate resolution
  • Microwave radiation cannot propagate beyond LEO altitude
  • Geostationary satellites cannot carry heavy instruments

Answers — Section 2

  1. B — Higher altitude means longer orbital period (Kepler’s third law) and worse spatial resolution (larger footprint).
  2. C — GSD = IFOV × altitude. The sensor’s angular resolution and distance to target are the primary determinants.
  3. C — GSD scales linearly with altitude: double the altitude = double the GSD (coarser resolution).
  4. B — A geostationary orbit is a special geosynchronous orbit in the equatorial plane with zero inclination.
  5. B — Microwave sensors require close proximity for adequate spatial resolution, necessitating low orbits.

Section 3 — Climate Feedbacks & Radiative Forcing

11. Which scenario describes a positive climate feedback loop?

  • Warming melts polar ice, lowering albedo, increasing absorption, causing further warming
  • Warmer temperatures increase low clouds that reflect sunlight and cool the surface
  • Increased CO₂ directly causes exactly 1 °C of warming with no further effects
  • Warming increases water vapor which forms more low clouds that cool the surface

12. Large volcanic eruptions inject sulfate aerosols into the stratosphere. This is an example of:

  • Positive radiative forcing (warming)
  • Negative radiative forcing (cooling)
  • Zero net radiative forcing
  • Positive feedback only, not forcing

13. How do clouds affect Earth’s radiative balance?

  • All clouds have the same net effect regardless of altitude
  • Low thick clouds mainly warm; high thin clouds mainly cool
  • Low thick clouds mainly cool by reflecting sunlight; high thin clouds mainly warm by trapping IR
  • Clouds play a negligible role compared to greenhouse gases

14. Earth’s surface temperature is ~15 °C while its effective radiating temperature is ~−18 °C. The ~33 °C difference represents:

  • Short-term climate variability from El Niño
  • The temperature difference between equator and poles
  • Measurement error in satellite instruments
  • The natural greenhouse effect of Earth’s atmosphere

15. If Earth emits less infrared energy to space than the solar energy it absorbs, what happens?

  • The average temperature decreases
  • The average temperature increases until a new equilibrium is reached
  • The temperature remains stable indefinitely
  • The atmosphere loses mass to space

Answers — Section 3

  1. A — The ice-albedo feedback is a classic positive feedback: warming → ice melt → lower albedo → more absorption → more warming.
  2. B — Stratospheric sulfate aerosols reflect incoming solar radiation, producing negative forcing (cooling for 1–3 years).
  3. C — Low thick clouds have high albedo (cooling); high thin clouds transmit SW but trap OLR (warming).
  4. D — The 33 °C difference is the warming produced by the natural greenhouse effect.
  5. B — An energy imbalance (more in than out) causes warming until outgoing radiation increases to match incoming.

Section 4 — Sea Level, Carbon Cycle & Applications

16. Which satellite missions are most directly used to monitor global sea level rise?

  • Radar altimeters on TOPEX/Poseidon, Jason series, and Sentinel-6
  • GRACE/GRACE-FO gravimetry missions only
  • MODIS multispectral imagers
  • OCO-2 atmospheric composition sensors

17. What are the two primary drivers of global mean sea level rise?

  • Groundwater pumping and increased rainfall over oceans
  • Melting sea ice and ocean basin expansion
  • Thermal expansion of seawater and melting land ice
  • Tidal forces from the Moon and tectonic activity

18. GRACE satellites can directly observe ice-mass loss because they measure:

  • Changes in surface albedo over ice sheets
  • Changes in Earth’s gravity field as mass is redistributed
  • The spectral signature of melting ice
  • Radar backscatter from ice surfaces

19. During strong El Niño events, atmospheric CO₂ rises faster than usual because:

  • Warmer oceans absorb excess carbon from the atmosphere
  • Tropical drought and heat suppress plant uptake and increase fires
  • The carbon cycle becomes insensitive to temperature changes
  • Increased cloud cover boosts photosynthesis globally

20. CERES measurements show decreased reflected shortwave radiation and nearly unchanged outgoing longwave radiation. This implies:

  • Earth is losing heat to space faster than before
  • Earth’s climate system is in perfect equilibrium
  • Earth is gaining heat — more solar energy absorbed with no compensating increase in OLR
  • Volcanic eruptions have increased global albedo

Answers — Section 4

  1. A — Radar altimeters on TOPEX/Poseidon, Jason, and Sentinel-6 directly measure sea surface height for sea level monitoring.
  2. C — Thermal expansion (warming water expands) and melting land ice (glaciers, ice sheets adding water) are the two main drivers.
  3. B — GRACE measures gravity field changes caused by mass redistribution, detecting ice-sheet and groundwater loss.
  4. B — El Niño causes tropical drought/heat that suppresses plant CO₂ uptake and triggers more biomass burning.
  5. C — Less reflected SW means more solar absorption; unchanged OLR means no compensating heat loss — Earth is gaining energy.
